EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

This report documents a complete research study examining how AI tool use 
affects high school students' creativity and sense of authorship. The study 
analyzed survey data from TWO datasets: v3_data.csv (212 participants) and 
v4_data.csv (246 participants), implementing rigorous statistical analyses 
including reliability testing, correlation analysis, multiple regression with 
controls, moderation analysis, and clustering.

KEY FINDINGS (Consistent across both datasets):
- AI use is negatively associated with creativity (r ≈ -0.31 to -0.34, p < 0.001)
- AI use is positively associated with authorship feelings (r ≈ +0.44, p < 0.001)
- Creativity and authorship are weakly negatively correlated (r ≈ -0.17 to -0.21, p < 0.05)
- These relationships persist after controlling for demographics, ability, workload, and context
- No significant moderation by writing ability (the AI-authorship relationship is consistent across all students)
- Three distinct student profiles identified through clustering

The analysis was conducted using Python (pandas, scipy, statsmodels, sklearn) 
in a Jupyter notebook environment. All statistical procedures followed best 
practices for psychological research.

PRIMARY CONSTRUCTS AND MEASUREMENT:

1. AI USE SCORE (AI_USE_SCORE)
   Definition: Frequency of AI tool use across five writing activities
   
   Items (5 items, all on 1-5 scale, "Never" to "Always"):
   1. "I use AI tools to brainstorm ideas for my school writing"
   2. "I use AI tools to help me draft or write full sentences and paragraphs"
   3. "I use AI tools to edit or proofread my writing"
   4. "I use AI tools when I am stuck and do not know how to continue"
   5. "Overall, I rely on AI tools when completing my writing assignments"
   
   Calculation: Mean of 5 items
   Reliability: v3 α = 0.890, v4 α = 0.888 (excellent, >0.80)
   Range: 1.000 to 5.000 (full scale range used)
   v3 Descriptive: M = 2.41, SD = 1.06
   v4 Descriptive: M = 2.46, SD = 1.06

2. CREATIVITY GENERAL (CREATIVITY_GENERAL)
   Definition: Students' general sense of creativity and confidence in 
   generating ideas independently, separate from AI assistance
   
   Items (3 items, all on 1-5 scale, "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree"):
   1. "My writing feels creative and original when I work on school assignments"
   2. "I feel confident in my own ability to generate creative ideas for 
      writing, even without AI"
   3. "I enjoy experimenting with different ways to express my ideas in writing"
   
   Note: Two other creativity items were excluded because they measure 
   AI-specific creativity enhancement ("AI helps me come up with new ideas" 
   and "My writing feels more creative with AI"), which is conceptually 
   distinct from general creativity.
   
   Calculation: Mean of 3 items
   Reliability: v3 α = 0.694, v4 α = 0.689 (acceptable, ~0.70)
   Range: 1.000 to 5.000
   v3 Descriptive: M = 3.74, SD = 0.84
   v4 Descriptive: M = 3.77, SD = 0.84

3. AUTHORSHIP SCORE (AUTHORSHIP_SCORE)
   Definition: Students' sense of ownership, authenticity, and connection to 
   their AI-assisted work
   
   Items (4 core items, all on 1-5 scale):
   1. "When I use AI tools, I still feel that the ideas in my writing belong 
      to me" (auth_ideas_mine) - positively worded
   2. "I feel comfortable taking credit for assignments where I used AI tools" 
      (auth_comfort_credit) - positively worded
   3. "When I use AI tools, I sometimes feel less connected to the writing as 
      'my' work" (auth_less_connected) - REVERSE CODED (6 - original score)
   4. "I worry that using AI tools might make my writing feel less genuine or 
      authentic" (auth_less_authentic) - REVERSE CODED (6 - original score)
   
   Reverse coding: Items 3 and 4 were reverse coded using: 
   reversed_score = 6 - original_score
   
   Excluded items:
   - "The work I submit feels like it is primarily my own" (auth_work_own): 
     Redundant with other items
   - "I worry that I'm using AI more than I should, and that I could get caught" 
     (auth_worry_copy): Measures different construct (worry about consequences) 
     - this was an extra question from the teacher
   
   Calculation: Mean of 4 items after reverse coding
   Reliability: v3 α = 0.748, v4 α = 0.738 (acceptable, ~0.75)
   Range: 1.000 to 5.000
   v3 Descriptive: M = 2.85, SD = 1.02
   v4 Descriptive: M = 2.85, SD = 1.00

CONTROL VARIABLES (for regression analyses):
- grade_num: Numeric grade level (9-12)
- gender_female: Dummy variable (0 = not female, 1 = female)
- writing_ability_num: Self-reported writing ability compared to peers 
  (1 = Much worse, 5 = Much better)
- assignments_per_week_num: Number of writing assignments per week
- overall_policy_num: School AI policy (1 = Completely not tolerated, 
  5 = Completely allowed)
- artificial_intelligence_instruction_num: Amount of AI education received 
  (1 = None at all, 5 = A lot)

================================================================================
ANALYSIS METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

All analyses were conducted using Python in a Jupyter notebook. The same 
methodology was applied to both v3 and v4 datasets.

PHASE 1: DATA PREPARATION
- Loaded CSV files with Timestamp as index
- Renamed columns to shorter, standardized names
- Calculated composite scores (means of item sets)
- Reverse coded negatively worded authorship items
- Converted categorical variables to numeric for regression

PHASE 2: RELIABILITY TESTING
Method: Cronbach's Alpha for internal consistency reliability
Formula: α = (k/(k-1)) × (1 - Σ(item variances)/total variance)
Interpretation:
- α ≥ 0.80: Excellent
- α ≥ 0.70: Acceptable
- α < 0.70: Questionable

Results (consistent across datasets):
- AI Use Scale: α ≈ 0.89 (excellent)
- Creativity General: α ≈ 0.69 (acceptable, slightly below ideal)
- Authorship Core: α ≈ 0.74 (acceptable, approaching good)

PHASE 3: CORRELATION ANALYSIS
Method: Pearson product-moment correlation (scipy.stats.pearsonr)
- Tests linear relationships between continuous variables
- Provides correlation coefficient (r) and p-value
- Effect sizes: |r| < 0.10 negligible, 0.10-0.29 small, 0.30-0.49 moderate, 
  0.50-0.69 large, ≥ 0.70 very large

PHASE 4: MULTIPLE REGRESSION ANALYSIS
Method: Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression (statsmodels.formula.api)

Model A: Predicting CREATIVITY_GENERAL
Formula: CREATIVITY = β₀ + β₁(AI_USE) + β₂(grade) + β₃(gender) + 
         β₄(writing_ability) + β₅(assignments) + β₆(policy) + 
         β₇(AI_education) + ε

Model B: Predicting AUTHORSHIP_SCORE
Formula: Same structure as Model A, different outcome

Purpose: Test whether AI use-outcome relationships persist after controlling 
for potential confounds (demographics, ability, workload, context)

Assumptions checked:
- Multicollinearity: All predictor correlations < 0.7 (no major concerns)
- Normality: Jarque-Bera test (Model A showed some non-normality, Model B 
  acceptable)
- Sample size: n = 211 (v3) after excluding cases with missing covariates

PHASE 5: MODERATION ANALYSIS
Method: Interaction term in regression model

Model C: Testing AI_USE × writing_ability interaction on AUTHORSHIP_SCORE
Formula: AUTHORSHIP = β₀ + β₁(AI_USE) + β₂(WRITING_ABILITY) + 
         β₃(AI_USE × WRITING_ABILITY) + controls + ε

Purpose: Test whether the AI-authorship relationship varies by writing ability
Interpretation: If β₃ is significant, the effect depends on writing ability

PHASE 6: CLUSTERING ANALYSIS (Exploratory)
Method: K-means clustering (sklearn.cluster.KMeans)
Features: AI_USE_SCORE, CREATIVITY_GENERAL, AUTHORSHIP_SCORE, 
          writing_ability_num, artificial_intelligence_instruction_num
Number of clusters: 3 (determined a priori)
Standardization: Features standardized using StandardScaler before clustering
Purpose: Identify distinct student profiles based on patterns across variables

REGRESSION RESULTS:

Model A (Predicting CREATIVITY_GENERAL):
- AI_USE_SCORE: β = -0.221, p < 0.001, R² = 0.250, n = 211
- Interpretation: After controlling for grade, gender, writing ability, 
  workload, school policy, and AI education, AI use still significantly 
  negatively predicts creativity. For each 1-point increase in AI use, 
  creativity decreases by 0.22 points (on 1-5 scale). The model explains 
  25% of variance in creativity.

Model B (Predicting AUTHORSHIP_SCORE):
- AI_USE_SCORE: β = +0.418, p < 0.001, R² = 0.238, n = 211
- Interpretation: After controlling for all covariates, AI use significantly 
  positively predicts authorship. For each 1-point increase in AI use, 
  authorship increases by 0.42 points. The model explains 24% of variance. 
  This is the key finding: students who use AI more feel MORE ownership, not 
  less.

MODERATION ANALYSIS (Model C):
- Interaction term (AI_USE × writing_ability): β = -0.019, p = 0.757
- Interpretation: No significant moderation. The positive relationship between 
  AI use and authorship is consistent across all levels of writing ability. 
  The effect does not depend on how skilled students perceive themselves to be.

================================================================================
THEORETICAL INTERPRETATION AND PRACTICAL MEANINGS
================================================================================

THE PARADOXICAL FINDING:
The most striking finding is the positive relationship between AI use and 
authorship feelings. This is counterintuitive - one might expect that using AI 
would make students feel LESS ownership of their work. Instead, students who 
use AI more report feeling MORE ownership.

POSSIBLE EXPLANATIONS:

1. AI AS TOOL, NOT REPLACEMENT:
   Students may view AI as a tool they control and direct, similar to how a 
   writer uses a thesaurus or grammar checker. They feel ownership because 
   they're making decisions about what AI generates and how to use it.

2. COGNITIVE DISSONANCE REDUCTION:
   Students who use AI frequently may engage in cognitive processes that 
   justify their use, leading them to emphasize their role in the process and 
   feel ownership.

3. SELECTION EFFECT:
   Students who are more comfortable with AI (and thus use it more) may also 
   be more comfortable claiming ownership of AI-assisted work. This could be a 
   personality or attitude difference rather than a causal effect of AI use.

4. DIFFERENT CONCEPTIONS OF AUTHORSHIP:
   Modern students may have different ideas about what "authorship" means in 
   the age of AI. They may see curation, editing, and direction as forms of 
   authorship even when AI generates initial content.

THE CREATIVITY FINDING:
The negative relationship between AI use and creativity is more intuitive but 
still important. Students who rely more on AI report feeling less creative 
independently. This could be:
- Causal: AI use reduces creative confidence
- Selection: Less creative students use AI more
- Both: A bidirectional relationship

THE WEAK CREATIVITY-AUTHORSHIP RELATIONSHIP:
The small negative correlation suggests these are somewhat independent 
constructs. Feeling creative doesn't necessarily mean feeling ownership, and 
vice versa.

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS:

1. FOR EDUCATORS:
   - Students using AI don't necessarily feel disconnected from their work
   - However, frequent AI use may be associated with reduced creative confidence
   - Need to help students develop both AI literacy AND independent creative skills

2. FOR POLICY:
   - Simply banning AI may not address the underlying issues
   - Students who use AI more feel ownership - this suggests they see it as 
     legitimate tool use
   - Policies should focus on teaching appropriate use rather than prohibition

3. FOR FUTURE RESEARCH:
   - Need longitudinal studies to establish causality
   - Need qualitative research to understand how students conceptualize 
     authorship with AI
   - Need to examine different types of AI use (brainstorming vs. full 
     generation) separately

================================================================================
LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
================================================================================

LIMITATIONS:

1. CROSS-SECTIONAL DESIGN:
   - Cannot establish causality - relationships are correlational
   - Don't know if AI use causes changes in creativity/authorship or if 
     pre-existing differences lead to different AI use patterns

2. SELF-REPORT MEASURES:
   - All measures are self-reported, subject to social desirability and 
     recall biases
   - Students may not accurately report AI use (underreporting due to 
     concerns about consequences)

3. SINGLE SCHOOL/CONTEXT:
   - Results may not generalize to other schools, regions, or educational 
     contexts
   - School policies and culture may influence findings

4. SAMPLE SIZE:
   - While adequate for most analyses (n = 212-246), larger samples would 
     provide more power
   - Some analyses (moderation) may have limited power

5. MEASUREMENT:
   - Creativity measure (α ≈ 0.69) is acceptable but not ideal
   - Some constructs may benefit from additional items or refinement

FUTURE DIRECTIONS:

1. LONGITUDINAL STUDIES:
   - Track students over time to establish causality
   - Examine how AI use patterns change and how this relates to creativity 
     and authorship

2. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGNS:
   - Randomly assign students to different AI use conditions
   - Test causal effects of AI use on outcomes

3. QUALITATIVE RESEARCH:
   - In-depth interviews to understand how students conceptualize authorship 
     with AI
   - Understand the decision-making process around AI use

4. REFINED MEASURES:
   - Develop and validate improved creativity measures
   - Create measures that distinguish different types of AI use (e.g., 
     brainstorming vs. full generation)

5. DIVERSE SAMPLES:
   - Replicate across different schools, regions, and educational levels
   - Examine whether relationships vary by context
